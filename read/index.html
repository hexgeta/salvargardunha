All 8 documents of the public consultation on Portugal's renewable-energy acceleration zones — the plan that puts the Serra da Gardunha in the most targeted region of the country. Each one summarised and rendered section by section in English, with the original Portuguese PDF linked.

Start Here: The ABC of the ZAER Public ConsultationJune 2026 · 13 pp The short introductory slide deck EMER 2030 published to open the public consultation on Portugal's Sectoral Programme for Renewable Energy Acceleration Zones (PSZAER) and its Strategic Environmental Assessment. It sets out the 2030 renewable targets, shows how far current installed capacity falls short of them, explains what changes inside an acceleration zone, lists the documents open for comment, and gives the timetable through to transposition into municipal plans in 2026–2028. It is the recommended starting point before the technical reports. The Proposal Itself: Sectoral Programme for Renewable Energy Acceleration Zones (PSZAER)May 2026 · 67 pp The central document — the proposed national programme designating Portugal's renewable-energy acceleration zones under EU Directive RED III. It maps roughly 578,777 ha as potential solar zones and 84,489 ha as wind zones, touching 193 municipalities for solar and 111 for wind. Inside those zones, projects would be licensed through a single-window procedure in which the usual project-level Environmental Impact Assessment is not carried out. The document states plainly that the great majority of the mapped areas, for both solar and wind, fall within the Pinhal do Centro landscape group — which includes the Serra da Gardunha. Non-Technical Summary of the Strategic Environmental AssessmentMay 2026 · 19 pp The plain-language version of the environmental assessment — written, in its own words, so that everyone who has the right to take part can understand what is being decided. It confirms that the programme maps where solar and wind projects could be licensed without an environmental impact assessment, and that after exclusions roughly 7% of mainland Portugal remains available. It openly records the risks: pressure on low-density rural territories, cumulative effects on landscape and biodiversity, and growing social contestation. Its conclusion is remarkable: local benefits tend to be greater when acceleration is done at smaller scale, closer to consumption. Thematic Report: Landscape and Cultural HeritageMay 2026 · 39 pp The landscape and cultural-heritage report behind the acceleration-zones programme. It sets out the legal framework, lists the heritage, agricultural and forestry categories excluded outright from the zones, then overlays the remaining potential zones onto Portugal's Landscape Unit Groups. The overlay finds that the “Pinhal do Centro” group — which expressly contains the landscape unit “Serras da Gardunha, Alvelos e Moradal” — stands out nationally: about 34% of it is potential solar zone, the highest share in the country. The report acknowledges that large-scale solar is experienced as significant visual degradation, creating “blankets of glass”, and proposes capping continuous panel areas at 25 ha. Thematic Report: EcologyMay 2026 · 70 pp The ecology chapter of the environmental assessment. It sets the criteria deciding which ecologically valuable areas stay out of the zones, combining protected areas (with a 1 km buffer), areas important for threatened species, and land uses with high biodiversity potential. It states plainly that wind turbines kill birds of prey, soaring birds and bats by blade collision, and that the power lines both technologies need kill birds by collision and electrocution. Crucially, it admits that information on where species occur outside protected areas is limited — which can undermine biodiversity protection exactly where our region sits. Thematic Report: Energy — “The barrier is the grid, not the land”May 2026 · 31 pp The energy chapter behind the programme — and quietly the most damaging document to the case for covering the interior in panels. Its central finding is that the main obstacle to renewables in Portugal is not a shortage of land or sunshine, but grid access and licensing: around 8.7 GW already holds grid reservations or connection agreements yet has never been connected. From this it concludes that acceleration zones should be small, modular and close to the grid and to consumption — on industrial, peri-urban and artificialised land — rather than large homogeneous blocks, which “accumulate constraints” and are less socially accepted. It cites roughly 28 GW of technical rooftop solar potential in urban areas. Legal Report: How Licensing Changes Inside a ZoneMay 2026 · 66 pp The legal report designing the licensing regime inside the acceleration zones. Its core proposal is a single-window procedure run by the energy directorate, in which the environmental assessment is done once, at plan level, and each individual project is then only checked for conformity with rules already set for the zone — dispensing, as a rule, with a full project-level environmental impact assessment. Binding deadlines apply (12 months; 6 for repowering) and administrative discretion is deliberately reduced. Its own comparative review concludes that speed comes not from removing environmental assessment but from moving it earlier — and warns Portugal may lack the institutional capacity to make that safe. The Government's Own Weighing of the Public Bodies' Opinions23 June 2026 · 47 pp The Government's own analysis of the 172 formal contributions made by the public bodies it consulted. Thirty-six bodies issued an opinion. Four were unfavourable: the cultural heritage authority, the agriculture authority, the national association of municipalities (unfavourable to both the content and the conduct of the process), and one municipality. Of the 172 contributions, 66 were accepted, 61 only partly accepted, and 45 merely “noted” — the formula used when an objection is recorded but changes nothing. Among those merely noted: the national planning authority's finding that the zones are far larger than the 2030 targets require.
