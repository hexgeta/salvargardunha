The Full Strategic Environmental Assessment

Avaliação Ambiental Estratégica — Relatório Ambiental Preliminar
Coordination: Maria do Rosário Partidário (Instituto Superior Técnico), for EMER 2030 · May 2026 · 249 pages

Summary

The complete environmental assessment — 249 pages, of which the short Non-Technical Summary is the public-facing version. It confirms that screening left roughly 7% of mainland Portugal open to projects that can proceed without an environmental impact assessment. Its value lies in what it openly concedes: that the pressure falls mainly on forest, farming and pasture land in low-density rural territories; that biodiversity values not currently known may exist inside the zones; that community opposition is legitimate; and that those who bear the territorial costs should be those who benefit.

What this assessment is for

Because a zone removes the project-level environmental assessment, this document is what replaces it. Its stated purpose includes generating guidance for projects that will be built in the absence of that assessment — which is the whole legal consequence of designating a zone.

It is explicitly not a project assessment. It evaluates a strategy, not the effects of a specific plant on a specific hillside. That gap is precisely what an objection can point to.

“[This assessment] will produce development contexts suited to future implementation proposals, and generate guidelines for the development of projects to be adopted in the face of the absence of EIA procedures.”p. 14

What it admits it does not know

The report's diagnosis of weaknesses is remarkably candid. It lists: poorly systematised knowledge about ecology, landscape and heritage; a lack of monitoring reports from existing projects; a lack of knowledge about the environmental impacts of projects already built, including mega power plants; unknown behavioural responses of protected fauna to this infrastructure; outdated municipal plans; land fragmentation and possible land speculation triggered by designating the zones; and growing social opposition.

In short: the Government proposes to remove project-level scrutiny while admitting it does not know what the projects already built have done.

“Lack of knowledge about the environmental impacts of existing projects, including mega power plants.”p. 37, among the principal weaknesses
“Incomplete knowledge about the geographic distribution of sensitive species and habitats; limited knowledge about the impacts of projects already built and about the effectiveness of the mitigation measures applied.”p. 63

Who carries the burden — and who gets the benefit

This is the heart of the document for anyone in the interior. It records that the mapped potential falls disproportionately on forest, agricultural, pasture and scrubland, meaning the transformation pressure lands on rural, low-density territories already carrying demographic and economic vulnerabilities.

It names the risks precisely: territorial concentration and monofunctionality; land speculation and land purchase by outside actors; value captured by actors outside the territory, reinforcing a perception of territorial extraction; and the reinforcement of inequality if the most fragile territories bear a disproportionate share of impacts without sufficient return.

“Part of the functional and landscape transformation pressure associated with acceleration may fall on rural and low-density territories, frequently already subject to demographic, economic and ecological vulnerabilities. This circumstance reinforces the importance of ensuring that those who bear the territorial costs of acceleration are those who benefit from it.”p. 103
“It is not enough to produce more energy to generate sustainable development at the local scale — it is necessary to consider who benefits, in which territories, at what scale, and with what concrete effects on people's lives.”pp. 59, 98

The “sacrificed territory” passage

On cumulative impacts, the report describes what happens when several projects pile into the same place. It is not a campaign slogan — it is the Government's own assessment describing the feeling it expects to create.

“It is no longer each isolated project that generates tension; it becomes the sum of their externalities — noise, traffic, visual effects, functional restrictions — that installs the feeling of a “sacrificed territory”, with direct consequences for the social contestation that the critical trends already identify as growing.”p. 59

Landscape: scale is the determining factor

The assessment states that impacts are generally directly proportional to project size and visibility, and that large-scale interventions in beautiful and functional landscapes carry significant risks of degrading both visual quality and the identity of the communities who live there.

It adds a nuance worth using: a small, badly designed project in a sensitive place can do more harm than a large, well-integrated one. Siting and design matter as much as size.

“All large-scale interventions, in themselves or through cumulative effect, in beautiful and functional landscapes that significantly alter that perception and landscape function carry significant risks of diminishing the visual quality of those landscapes, the identity matrices of the communities that build and enjoy them, and the compatible economic activities that develop there.”pp. 67–68

Opposition is legitimate — the assessment says so

In its conclusions, the report lists what may limit acceleration. Alongside the grid, project economics and municipal planning, it names community opposition — and it chooses the word legitimately.

This single line is worth quoting in an objection: the Government's own environmental assessment accepts that refusing large projects near your home is a legitimate position, not obstruction.

“[Among the aspects that may limit acceleration:] the opposition of communities that legitimately refuse the existence of large projects in their vicinity.”p. 99

Excluding sensitive areas is not enough — it says that too

The report explicitly refuses the argument that, because sensitive areas were excluded, projects inside the zones are safe. Excluding areas is only the first step of the mitigation hierarchy — and unknown biodiversity values may exist inside the zones.

It concedes there is a relevant incidence on natural space inside the zones, and that uncertainty remains about its sensitivity.

“Although the exercise of excluding areas relevant to biodiversity and landscape may be considered the first step of the mitigation hierarchy (avoiding impacts), the implementation of measures and guidelines aimed at mitigating possible unforeseen impacts is not considered dispensable (for example, biodiversity values that are not known may occur in the region of the ZAER).”pp. 99–100
“Since, with the ZAER identified, there is nonetheless a relevant incidence on natural space — whose sensitivity will not be significant but about which uncertainty also exists — it is important to guard against potential negative impacts.”p. 100

What it recommends

Mechanisms to control territorial concentration and cumulative impacts; preventing disproportionately burdened territories and energy monofunctionality; favouring repowering and reuse of existing infrastructure before opening new territorial fronts; preventing land speculation; requiring local benefit plans that are tangible, equitable and verifiable; setting maximum project density limits inside zones; landscape observatories with photographic monitoring; and public participation that is early, informed and has real influence over project definition.

It recommends the programme be implemented in phases, so it can be adjusted as reality unfolds.

The public consultation is how this gets challenged. Objections are part of the official decision process and must be considered by law.
