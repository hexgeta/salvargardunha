Thematic Report: Landscape and Cultural Heritage

Relatório Temático – Paisagem e Património Cultural
BIODESIGN, for Instituto Superior Técnico (coordination: Jorge Cancela) · May 2026 · 39 pages

Summary

The landscape and cultural-heritage report behind the acceleration-zones programme. It sets out the legal framework, lists the heritage, agricultural and forestry categories excluded outright from the zones, then overlays the remaining potential zones onto Portugal's Landscape Unit Groups. The overlay finds that the “Pinhal do Centro” group — which expressly contains the landscape unit “Serras da Gardunha, Alvelos e Moradal” — stands out nationally: about 34% of it is potential solar zone, the highest share in the country. The report acknowledges that large-scale solar is experienced as significant visual degradation, creating “blankets of glass”, and proposes capping continuous panel areas at 25 ha.

Why landscape is contested

The report opens by acknowledging that the roll-out of renewable energy has become a live technical and social controversy. Many installations are at a scale and in locations that readily become contentious because of visual intrusion and the loss of identity tied to traditional land uses that affected communities are used to.

Because landscape is understood as the historical reflection of social and economic life in the territory, cultural, archaeological and built heritage are folded into the same theme.

“Many of the interventions have a scale and a location that easily become controversial because of the significant visual intrusion and the loss of identity with the more traditional land uses to which the affected communities are accustomed.”section 1

The legal framework: landscape as a right

Portugal approved the European Landscape Convention in 2005 (later the Council of Europe Landscape Convention). It defines landscape as an area perceived by people, whose character results from natural and/or human action — making “character” the essential value to be identified.

Under the Convention, Portugal committed to recognising landscape in law, establishing landscape policies, guaranteeing public participation in them, and integrating landscape into all other public policies. The Convention's “landscape quality objectives” are explicitly defined as the aspirations of the population for their own surroundings, arrived at after public consultation — which is precisely what a consultation like this one is for.

“[Landscape quality objectives] may be expressed as the formulation of the aspirations of the populations with regard to the landscape features of their living environment, for each of the landscapes identified and assessed, and after a process of public consultation.”section 2, quoting the Landscape Convention

The report's own warning about large-scale intervention

Landscape, cultural heritage and biodiversity are merged into a single Critical Decision Factor. The premise is that large renewable projects have a strong territorial footprint and can significantly alter the structure, character and visual quality of landscapes.

The report is direct about the risk in valued landscapes — while noting that already-degraded, mono-functional sites, brownfields, disused mines and industrial land are where renewables are an opportunity. It also cautions that scale alone does not determine harm: a small, badly sited project can do more damage than a larger, well-integrated one.

It draws a pointed distinction between genuine agrivoltaics and solar parks where grazing is really just an undergrowth-maintenance measure rather than a pre-existing farming activity.

“all large-scale interventions, in themselves or through cumulative effect, in beautiful and functional landscapes (rural or urban) that significantly alter that landscape perception and function, carry significant risks of diminishing the visual quality of those landscapes, the identity matrices of the communities that build and enjoy them, and the compatible economic activities that develop there.”section 3.3

What was excluded from the zones before they were drawn

The landscape and heritage exclusions are: classified heritage (and heritage being classified) with its protection zones; cultural landscapes classified as UNESCO World Heritage; FAO Globally Important Agricultural Heritage Systems; archaeological heritage plus a 150 m buffer; and geosites of recognised geological value.

The National Agricultural Reserve (RAN) is also excluded on grounds of its agri-food productivity, described as a scarce resource in Portugal. Specific land uses are excluded too: rice fields; all cork oak and holm oak cover; all other oaks, chestnuts and broadleaves; and all stone pine.

Further exclusions cover a 2 km coastal strip and areas of forestry interest, including Trees of Public Interest and Integrated Landscape Management Areas.

The overlay — and why the Gardunha is named

The exclusion mapping was crossed with Portugal's Landscape Unit Groups. For solar, Pinhal do Centro is by a wide margin the most affected group in the country: 1,371.82 km² of its 4,014.86 km², or 34.17%. The next groups are Beira Alta at 15.16% and Beira Litoral at 11.88%. The Algarve is lowest at 0.08%. For wind, Pinhal do Centro is again the highest at 6.57%.

The report expressly lists the landscape units that make up this group: “63 – Pinhal Interior”, “64 – Vale do Zêzere”, “65 – Serras da Gardunha, Alvelos e Moradal” and “66 – Maciço agro-florestal – Castelo Branco”. This is the passage in which the Serra da Gardunha is named in the Government's own documents as part of the most targeted landscape group in Portugal.

The report then explains why this area came out on top — and the reasoning is worth reading carefully. Because every culturally and scenically higher-value area was excluded first, what remains is, by construction, the land the method rates lowest. It cites an academic source to describe the area as “far from a sustainable landscape”.

“These analyses show that around 40% of the territory included in the Landscape Unit Group “J – Pinhal do Centro”, which thus stands out within the national set, has potential as ZAER, both for solar (c. 34% of that Group) and wind (likewise, c. 6%).”section 5
“It is therefore natural that the remaining territorial areas are those with the lowest current landscape-quality values, such as those included in this Landscape Unit Group.”section 5

The 25-hectare cap and what it really costs in land

The report's headline management rule: continuous areas of solar panels may not exceed 25 ha. Beyond that, landscape compartmentalisation must surround the entire continuous panel area — following ridgelines and thalwegs, roughly 40 m wide, with tree-and-shrub screens of native or traditional species (never invasive ones).

Where the perimeter is directly visible from a road, the screen is replaced by an embankment about 2 m high and 15 m wide. Panels must follow existing contours, avoiding earthworks that alter the terrain profile and drainage.

The land arithmetic is striking: the report quantifies the additional area needed for compartmentalisation and integration at around 30–35% of the panel area — so 100 ha of panels requires a further 30 to 35 ha on top.

“Continuous areas of solar panels may not exceed 25 ha. Beyond this dimension, landscape compartmentalisation measures must be established, surrounding the entire continuous area of panels.”section 7.1

Other project measures for solar and wind

The measures include: archaeological monitoring during works; continuous vegetation screening sensitive viewpoints, roads, heritage areas and settlements; herbaceous cover and/or grazing under panels to avoid bare soil; a plant maintenance plan with establishment irrigation, replacement planting and invasive removal; support buildings painted in earth, dry-green or grey tones; the lowest-reflectance panel technology available; buried internal cabling; aviation warning lights activated only on aircraft detection; new power lines routed along existing corridors or mid-slope, avoiding ridgelines and silhouettes against the sky; and a landscape monitoring plan with periodic photographic reports.

The conclusion: “blankets of glass”

The conclusion accepts the case for renewables, and notes these technologies are well accepted at small scale and in self-consumption — but are strongly contested in centralised, large-scale systems that significantly alter the perception and functioning of the rural landscape.

It distinguishes wind from solar. Wind meets less opposition through habituation to an essentially vertical technology. Solar is different — and here the report's own language is the most quoted line in this whole consultation.

“[Solar], by occupying large areas of horizontal extent, with reflections of light, creating blankets of glass over essentially flattened or slightly undulating territories, is felt very strongly as a significant visual degradation, leading many communities of observers to react negatively to the introduction of this technology at large scales.”section 8
“the solution will be, first, to avoid the areas which for various reasons are considered of great collective landscape and heritage interest; and second, to establish a maximum dimension for large-scale solar panel parks (in the case of the ZAER, 25 ha is proposed), compartmentalised by naturalised, biophysically diversified structures.”section 8

The public consultation is how this gets challenged. Objections are part of the official decision process and must be considered by law.
